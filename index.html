Uber Demand Forecasting:
From Historical Data to Driver Deployment

A complete demand forecasting pipeline — data simulation, model backtesting, concert-night prediction, and an operations memo — built from scratch in Python.

01

The Business Problem

Every minute of every day, Uber has to answer one question before anything else matters: how many people are going to want a ride, and where? Get it right and drivers are waiting when riders open the app — pickups are fast, riders are happy, drivers earn steadily, and the business works. Get it wrong and everything breaks at once. Too few drivers means five-minute wait times stretch to twenty, riders cancel and call a cab, and Uber loses revenue it can never recover. Too many drivers means cars circling empty blocks burning gas and patience, drivers earning nothing, and the best ones switching to Lyft by next week. Demand forecasting isn't a feature of Uber's business — it is the business. The algorithm that predicts how many rides are coming in the next hour is the single most consequential piece of software the company runs, because every pricing decision, every driver notification, and every surge multiplier flows directly from that number.

02

The Data and the Model

I started by generating two years of historical Friday night Uber trip data (104 weeks) for the area around WWU's campus in Bellingham, Washington. The dataset captures four real demand drivers that Uber's own models account for: a long-run growth trend as Uber's market share expands, summer seasonality from tourism, a school-year effect when 16,000 WWU students are in town, and the annual New Year's Eve spike. I then split the data — training a linear regression model on the first 78 weeks and hiding the final 26 weeks as a blind backtest. The model predicted those unseen Fridays with a Mean Absolute Error of roughly 27 trips, about 6.5% of average demand — accurate enough to trust for operational planning.

The model's predictions (orange dashed line) tracked the general level of demand through the backtest window, staying within the ±15% tolerance band for most weeks. However, it missed the sharp spikes in November and late December — weeks where actual trips jumped to 480, well above the model's flat prediction line. The model captures trend and seasonality but smooths over the short-term volatility that matters most on the busiest nights.

The dashed line is what the model predicted. The solid line is what actually happened. The gap between them is the model's error — and the honest measure of how much to trust it.

04

Stress-Testing the Forecast

Before sending that memo, I stress-tested the key assumptions to understand how fragile the recommendation was.

The surge pricing recommendation stayed on for every scenario tested — even with drivers completing 5 trips per hour and 60 drivers online at 10pm, demand (977 trips in the high scenario) overwhelmed supply (300 trip capacity) by a 677-trip shortfall. The recommendation to activate surge pricing is not fragile; it holds under every plausible combination of assumptions. The question isn't whether to surge — it's how aggressively to recruit additional drivers beforehand.

Stress-testing is what separates a number from a recommendation. Any model can produce a point estimate — 580 trips, 194 drivers — but an operations manager can't act on a single number without knowing how it breaks. By varying the assumptions that matter most (the concert surge multiplier, the trips-per-driver rate), we find out whether the recommendation flips under plausible conditions or holds firm. A recommendation that survives stress-testing earns trust; one that doesn't gets flagged with guardrails before it reaches a decision-maker.

05

When the Algorithm Decides

In production, Uber's system doesn't wait for a human to read the memo. The forecast feeds directly into the surge pricing algorithm, which sets the multiplier automatically — no review, no approval, no delay. When the model is right, this is a superpower: prices adjust in real time, drivers are summoned to exactly the right intersection, and thousands of riders get home safely after the concert. But when the model is wrong — say an earthquake shuts down roads and the model still thinks it's a normal Friday — that same automation becomes a liability. Surge prices spike on stranded people, drivers are routed into impassable streets, and the algorithm optimizes confidently for a world that no longer exists. The speed that makes automated decisions powerful is the same speed that makes them dangerous.

06

When the Model Breaks

Six weeks after the concert, three things happen at once: WWU's spring enrollment drops 12% as students transfer or take gap quarters, a new apartment complex opens in the Cordata neighborhood (shifting where riders live), and Lyft launches in Bellingham with first-ride promos. The model has never seen any of this. It was trained on a world where enrollment grew, Cordata was empty, and Uber had no local competitor — so it keeps predicting as if that world still exists. Drivers get dispatched to the wrong neighborhoods and earn less per hour. Riders near Cordata wait longer because the model doesn't know they're there, and some quietly switch to Lyft. This is distribution shift: the real world moved and nobody told the model. Nobody told the drivers or riders why things got worse, either — they just experienced it.

07

My Analysis

The data we use to initially train the model in the beginning shapes the predictions made by the model in the end. All the trends and spikes in activity found in the initial first two years led to both accurate predictions as well as miscalculations. This was especially apparent when trying to predict a concert night. The model heavily underestimated the expected amount of traffic and could have led to a major problem if human intervention didn't take place. I also saw the limits of the model when major changes to the environment occur — such as the appearance of a competitor — and the model slowly devolves, making less and less accurate predictions. Overall, if I were to build a similar system for a business in Bellingham, I would add multiple triggers that lead to human review to prevent the model from fueling misinformed decisions.
